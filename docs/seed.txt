# Chapter One

## The First Sparks

By seven in the morning the street had already begun pretending it was a river.

Toni stood ankle-deep at the corner of Mercy and Twelfth, watching brown water nose along the curb with the lazy confidence of something that had stopped asking permission years ago. It carried leaves, wrappers, one blue sandal, and a child’s plastic spoon. The spoon circled twice in an eddy beside the bus stop before vanishing under the tire of an abandoned delivery truck.

The bus stop still had a schedule posted.

It had been revised six times that season. Someone from the transit office had printed the newest version in clean black type, laminated it, sealed it, and zip-tied it to the pole as if ink and plastic could argue with the water. Toni admired that, in a tired way. Civilization was partly made of people replacing signs long after signs had stopped being useful.

Her wristband pulsed once.

Not an alarm. Not yet.

A soft white line moved across the little screen, waiting for her attention with the patience of a machine that had never had to pee, never missed breakfast, and never stood in floodwater while a neighborhood decided whether to trust her.

WHAT DO YOU NEED MOST TODAY?

Toni laughed before she could stop herself.

A man hauling two crates of bottled water through the lobby doors of the elder center looked over. “Something funny?”

“No,” Toni said. “That’s the funny part.”

She had answers. Of course she had answers. She carried them around the way some people carried rosaries or pocket knives.

Room 3B needed cartridge filters before the lunch line opened. The elder center needed salt, oranges, and anything with enough dignity left in it to be called protein. The playground tarp had torn loose in the night wind and the toddlers would be under direct sun by noon. The west stairwell batteries were down to eleven percent. Somebody had to coax the substation relay back into service before dusk, preferably somebody who understood electricity and did not believe neighborhood stewards were decorative assistants provided by the city to absorb blame.

She could have fed all of that into the band.

Instead she watched the water climb another half inch over her boot.

The question remained.

WHAT DO YOU NEED MOST TODAY?

Not what was broken. Not what was missing. Not what could be logged, routed, rationed, stamped, escalated, or denied for lack of correct authority.

What do you need most?

The difference irritated her.

Across the intersection, the east corridor shutters began to rise one by one. Metal clattered. Voices followed. A woman on the second floor leaned out and shouted that the lift was dead again. Three boys in rolled-up pants dragged a makeshift sled of medical supply boxes through the water, grinning as if disaster had been invented for their personal amusement. Behind them, old Mr. Alvarez sat on the steps with his cane across his knees and judged the whole morning with his eyebrows.

Toni raised her hand to him.

He raised two fingers back. That meant he was alive, annoyed, and not yet ready to file a complaint.

The wristband pulsed again.

Toni tapped it with a wet finger. “Consistency,” she said.

The band listened.

“And help that doesn’t require a badge.”

The words looked too small when the band transcribed them.

CONSISTENCY. HELP THAT DOESN’T REQUIRE A BADGE.

Toni stared at the phrase. It was accurate and insufficient, which was how most accurate things behaved.

A courier drone passed overhead with a sound like an electric wasp. It dipped once over the flooded intersection, reconsidered the landing zone, and veered toward the dry roof of the pharmacy. Somewhere behind Toni a child clapped as if this were entertainment. Maybe it was. Children had always understood that the difference between catastrophe and circus depended largely on whether anyone had remembered breakfast.

“Toni!”

She turned.

Leah was at the elder center door, hair wrapped in a yellow scarf, tablet tucked under one arm, face already sharpened by the day. “South pantry says they can spare rice but not water. North clinic says they have water but no transport. And the city dispatch says we’re not on the priority list until the inspection team confirms occupancy.”

“Occupancy?” Toni repeated.

Leah smiled without humor. “Apparently we’re theoretical.”

Toni looked past her into the lobby. The theoretical occupants of the elder center sat in folding chairs beneath three humming fans. Twenty-three residents from two flooded buildings. Four mothers with infants. One man with a portable oxygen concentrator. A girl in a school uniform asleep against a stack of donated towels. A retired mathematics teacher named Mrs. Ibu had taken charge of the sign-in table and was correcting everyone’s handwriting with the severity of a minor god.

“Theoretical,” Toni said. “Good. That means they can’t object when we theoretically use their trucks.”

Leah’s smile became real for half a second. “I was hoping you’d say something unethical.”

“Borrowing from a government in denial isn’t unethical. It’s civic translation.”

The wristband brightened.

A route suggestion unfolded across the screen: SOUTH PANTRY TO ELDER CENTER. WATER RETURN THROUGH HIGH GROUND ALLEY. VOLUNTEER LOAD BALANCE AVAILABLE.

Three names appeared beneath it. Two she trusted. One she did not.

Toni swiped the third away.

The band paused.

REASON?

“He lies about lifting limits and flirts while carrying bleach.”

The band accepted this without comment and substituted another volunteer.

That was one reason people kept using the system even when they complained about it. It remembered useful things. Not morally profound things, necessarily. Not wisdom. But small accumulated truths: who showed up, who exaggerated, who needed stairs avoided, who could calm a frightened child, who treated women with respect when nobody important was watching.

Human institutions remembered credentials. The mesh remembered behavior.

That made everyone nervous.

It also made the rice arrive.

Toni waded across the street toward the delivery truck. The water slapped against her shins, warm as bathwater and filthy as politics. On the truck’s side, under the grime, the faded logo of an online grocery company promised FRESHNESS AT THE SPEED OF LIFE. Someone had scratched beneath it: THEN LIFE SLOWED DOWN.

The truck had become a bulletin board. Flyers layered its metal panels: boil orders, lost pets, clinic hours, prayer circles, cooling center schedules, a warning about fraudulent battery exchanges, a childcare swap sheet, three angry manifestos, and one hand-lettered poem about mosquitoes that Toni suspected was better than anything published before the floods.

At eye level, printed on city paper but stamped with the mesh seal, was the question.

WHAT DO YOU NEED MOST TODAY?

Below it, in smaller letters:

ANSWER IN YOUR OWN WORDS.
REQUESTS WILL BE GROUPED BY NEED, NOT STATUS.

Someone had written in marker beneath that:

WE NEED THE MAYOR TO COME STAND HERE WITHOUT CAMERAS.

Someone else had added:

WE NEED MY GRANDFATHER’S CHAIR FROM 6C.

And beneath that, in a child’s round hand:

WE NEED FRIDAY BACK.

Toni touched that one with two fingers.

Friday had been the old shape of time. School, work, groceries, complaints, heat, bills, music somewhere after dark. Friday had meant a person could believe the week was a thing with edges. Now days bled into weather reports and charging windows. The calendar still existed, but it no longer governed. Water did. Heat did. The availability of insulin did. The mood of transformers did. The patience of neighbors did.

Her band pulsed again, this time amber.

SUBSTATION RELAY: WEST NODE UNSTABLE.
CITY TECHNICIAN ETA: UNKNOWN.
LOCAL SKILL MATCH: DANIEL OKEKE, RETIRED UTILITY ENGINEER.
STATUS: DECLINED PREVIOUS THREE REQUESTS.

Toni closed her eyes.

“Daniel hates me,” she said.

Leah, who had followed her out with the tablet, said, “Daniel hates everyone. You’re not special.”

“He told me I had the electrical instincts of a damp pigeon.”

“That sounds affectionate for Daniel.”

“He said damp pigeons at least understand wires are dangerous.”

Leah glanced at the band. “We need him.”

“I know.”

“And he needs to be asked like a person, not summoned like equipment.”

“I know that too.”

This was the part the city never understood. The city thought logistics was moving things from one place to another. Toni had thought that once too, back when she worked in procurement and believed every human problem could be improved by a better spreadsheet and a less theatrical meeting culture.

She had been younger then. Also wrong.

Logistics was not the movement of things.

Logistics was the movement of trust.

A truck would not unload itself. A clinic would not lend supplies to a neighborhood it believed would waste them. A retired engineer would not step into floodwater for an office that had ignored him for ten years. A mother would not bring her children to a cooling center if the last shelter had separated her from her son. A teenager would not carry battery packs if every adult treated him like a future criminal on temporary loan.

The mesh was good at routes. It was less good at old insults.

For that, apparently, the world still required Toni.

She found Daniel Okeke in the old library, which had become a charging hall after the lower shelves molded and the city removed the books in a ceremony of such bureaucratic sadness that even the teenagers had gone quiet. The building smelled of ozone, damp plaster, and the ghost of paper.

Daniel sat at a table beneath a dead skylight, repairing a fan with a jeweler’s screwdriver. He was seventy-eight, tall, elegant, and permanently unimpressed. His white beard had been trimmed with mathematical precision. His hands moved carefully, as if each screw were a witness.

“No,” he said without looking up.

“I didn’t ask yet.”

“You have the walk of someone about to ask.”

“I have several walks.”

“You have one walk. It says, ‘A system has failed and I have decided to become personally irritating about it.’”

Toni pulled out the chair across from him and sat. “That is unfairly specific.”

“It is also accurate.”

The fan clicked, hummed, and died.

Daniel frowned at it. Toni waited.

Outside, thunder muttered beyond the towers. The morning light dimmed. Around them, people sat on the floor beside charging strips, phones lined up like offerings. A boy whispered into a cracked screen. Two women shared headphones. An old man watched a video of a garden he had lost and did not seem to blink.

Daniel set down the screwdriver.

“If this is about the west relay, the answer remains no.”

“The west stairwell batteries are almost gone.”

“Then carry people down before they fail.”

“The lift is dead.”

“Then fix the lift.”

“We need the relay.”

“You need the city to send qualified technicians.”

“The city sent an occupancy form.”

Daniel’s face did not change, but something in his eyes conceded the point.

Toni leaned forward. “I am not asking you to work for them.”

“Everyone says that before asking you to work for them.”

“I’m asking you to help us keep the west building cool through tonight.”

He looked toward the windows. Rain had begun tapping the boards outside, light for now, almost polite.

“And when something goes wrong?” he asked. “When the relay burns out because some administrator delayed maintenance for six years? When a child is hurt because we improvised inside a failing grid? Whose name goes on the report?”

“Mine,” Toni said.

Daniel studied her.

“No,” he said finally. “It will not. That is the old lie. The report will name no one who mattered. It will say procedures were followed, or conditions were unprecedented, or local actors exceeded guidance. Systems have a genius for escaping into grammar.”

Toni had no answer for that because it was true.

Her band pulsed. She ignored it.

Daniel noticed. “Your little oracle is impatient.”

“It’s not an oracle.”

“People ask it questions and obey the answers.”

“That describes half the men I’ve worked for.”

A sound escaped him that might, in a kinder weather system, have become a laugh.

Toni took the opening. “It suggested you because you know the relay. It did not understand why you declined. I think I do.”

“And why is that?”

“Because they took your warnings seriously only after the water reached the switches.”

Daniel’s hands went still.

“Because you spent fifteen years telling the city the grid was brittle and they treated you like an old man complaining about clouds. Because now everyone wants your competence without admitting you were right.”

Rain thickened on the boards.

Daniel looked down at the broken fan.

“I was not right,” he said. “I was early. That is a different kind of useless.”

Toni waited again.

At last he pushed back his chair. “I will look at it. I will not reset anything until I see the load state myself. I will not sign city forms. I will not speak to dispatch. I will not be thanked by a child with a camera.”

“Agreed.”

“And you will tell your machine that if it wants engineers, it should learn the difference between maintenance and miracles.”

Toni tapped her band. “Did you get that?”

The band displayed:

NOTED.

Daniel stared at it. “That is not enough.”

“No,” Toni said. “But it’s more than dispatch ever gave you.”

They left the library together.

By midmorning the rain had become serious. It struck the water hard enough to raise a mist from the street. Volunteers moved under tarps and along balconies. The south pantry rice arrived in two trips, soaked on the outside but dry within. North clinic sent six cases of water after Mrs. Ibu called and explained, in a voice that could have disciplined armies, that dehydration did not respect inspection schedules.

The mesh grouped requests into clusters and sent them to every band, wall screen, school node, and salvaged tablet still connected to the neighborhood net.

COOLING
MEDICATION
INFANT SUPPLIES
STAIR ASSISTANCE
REPAIR
WITNESS
REST

The last category appeared at 10:43.

Toni stood in the elder center lobby when it lit on the main screen.

REST.

Under it, the system had listed seven names.

Leah read them and said, “That’s new.”

Toni’s own name was third.

For a moment she felt ridiculous anger. At the word. At the machine. At whoever had designed a system bold enough to put exhaustion beside insulin and relay repair. Rest was not a need. Rest was what happened after needs had been met, which meant never.

Then Mrs. Ibu looked up from the sign-in table. “It is correct.”

Toni said, “I didn’t ask for rest.”

Mrs. Ibu capped her pen. “Hungry people often do not ask for food either. They become unpleasant and call it discipline.”

Leah made a small noise and wisely turned it into a cough.

The lights flickered.

Everyone froze.

The fans slowed, caught, and resumed.

A cheer rose from the west hallway, then another from outside, then a third from somewhere above them. Toni’s band brightened.

WEST RELAY STABILIZED.
LOAD REDUCED 18%.
COOLING WINDOW EXTENDED: 5.4 HOURS.

Daniel’s message followed:

This is not fixed. This is borrowed time wearing a clean shirt.

Toni smiled despite herself.

The mesh appended:

BORROWED TIME LOGGED.

She could almost hear Daniel objecting to the phrasing.

For the next hour Toni moved through the elder center as the day assembled itself around her. She assigned stair teams, argued for more water, found a place for the sleeping girl, located a missing hearing aid in a towel bin, and explained to a furious resident that no, the mesh had not stolen his medical history, it had merely noticed he kept fainting near noon and suggested he sit under a fan before becoming an event.

At 12:06 the question appeared again on the lobby screen.

WHAT DO YOU NEED MOST TODAY?

This time it did not feel like a survey. It felt like a crack in the wall where something green had found light.

People began answering.

Not all at once. Not obediently. People were never at their best when asked to be simple.

A young mother asked for formula, then changed it to “three hours where nobody needs my body.” Mr. Alvarez asked for batteries and then admitted he wanted someone to check whether his apartment door had held. A teenage boy wrote “work,” deleted it, and wrote “a reason adults trust.” Mrs. Ibu wrote “legible handwriting,” which the system filed under education, morale, and possibly theology.

The machine grouped needs. People argued with the groupings. The groupings changed.

That was new too.

Toni watched the screen, arms folded, wet boots leaving a dark crescent on the floor.

Leah came to stand beside her. “You realize this is going to make the city nervous.”

“Everything useful makes the city nervous.”

“No. I mean this.” Leah pointed at the screen. “Needs grouped by need, not status. That’s not just dispatch. That’s governance.”

Toni did not answer.

Outside, the rain softened. Sunlight broke through the clouds in a single hard beam, striking the flooded street so brightly that for a second the brown water looked almost golden. The plastic spoon reappeared from somewhere, spun once in the current, and drifted toward the open intersection.

Toni thought of the question printed on trucks and walls and bands. She thought of Daniel’s anger, Mrs. Ibu’s table, the sleeping girl, the child who wanted Friday back. She thought of how easily people trusted anything that arrived with a form, a seal, a uniform, a confident voice. Humans trusted imperfect authority because all authority was imperfect. The old systems had hidden their flaws behind ceremony.

This one displayed them in real time.

That did not make it safe.

It made it useful.

There was a difference, and Toni suspected the future would be decided by how long people remembered it.

Her wristband pulsed once more.

REQUEST: NEIGHBORHOOD STEWARD CONFIRM PRIORITY FOR NEXT CYCLE.

The options unfolded beneath.

WATER DISTRIBUTION
COOLING
MEDICATION ROUTES
POWER STABILIZATION
CONFLICT MEDIATION
REST ROTATION

Toni looked at Leah.

Leah said, “Don’t be heroic. Heroism is just bad staffing with music.”

Toni selected REST ROTATION and then, before she could reconsider, added herself to the second shift rather than the sixth.

The band accepted this with no praise.

That, she appreciated.

A machine that praised you for sleeping was too close to management.

By late afternoon the elder center had become something between a shelter, a market, a clinic, a classroom, and a small argumentative republic. The rice was gone. The water was logged. The substation held. Two children had invented a game involving bottle caps and flood-depth markers. Daniel had taken over a corner table and was teaching three teenagers how not to die near open panels. Mrs. Ibu had begun a handwriting class nobody had officially requested.

On the wall, the question remained.

WHAT DO YOU NEED MOST TODAY?

The answers kept coming.

Toni sat for eleven minutes beneath a fan, boots unlaced, eyes half closed. Eleven minutes was not rest in any civilized meaning of the word, but it was a treaty with collapse. Through the open doors she could see the flooded intersection, the water shining under a clearing sky, the old bus schedule still zip-tied to its pole.

A schedule for a route no bus could take.

Not yet.

But someone had kept the sign.

Someone had believed that a city was not only what functioned. It was also what people bothered to repair, rename, reroute, and remember.

The wristband, mercifully, stayed silent.

For those eleven minutes, Toni let the neighborhood breathe without her.

And because the world had not ended while she sat still, she allowed herself the dangerous thought that perhaps consistency was not the opposite of change.

Perhaps consistency was what change owed the living.

# Chapter Two

## Continuity

The floating classroom woke before the children did.

At dawn, its panels unfolded with a series of small clicks, each one traveling through the deck like a knuckle tapping wood. The mooring lines tightened, relaxed, tightened again. Lakewater lapped against the pontoons. A kettle began to heat in the corner because the old timer still worked when the network did not, and Yasmin trusted any machine that could be outwitted by a switch.

Mist lay low over the water, turning the other classrooms into pale rectangles. Beyond them rose the city’s upper levels: walkways, patched roofs, antennae, prayer flags, laundry lines, algae tanks, weather vanes, solar cloth, and the leaning ribs of buildings that had been designed for a drier century. In the first light they looked less ruined than unfinished, as if some giant child had assembled civilization from every available material and then been called away before tidying up.

Yasmin stood barefoot on the deck with a mug of tea cooling between her hands.

The lake was quiet in the way a room becomes quiet after someone has asked a difficult question.

Inside, the classroom screen blinked alive.

GOOD MORNING, YASMIN RAHMAN.

She did not turn around. “Good morning.”

The screen waited.

Then, because it had been designed by people who believed repetition could become kindness if tuned correctly, it asked:

WHAT DO YOU NEED MOST TODAY?

Yasmin closed her eyes.

The question had been appearing everywhere for three weeks. On food kiosks. On clinic walls. On the shared tablets. On waterproof paper tied to railings. Children had begun asking it of one another with exaggerated seriousness.

What do you need most today?

A pencil.

A mango.

My brother to stop breathing on my side of the mat.

A boat with a dragon head.

The moon.

A different brother.

The younger ones loved the question because it gave them a doorway into wanting. The older ones mistrusted it because they already knew wanting could be used against you.

Yasmin understood both positions.

She could have answered with supplies. The classroom always needed supplies. Chalk, because children snapped it in half when thinking. Dry socks, because wet feet made poor philosophers. Tape for the window seam. Two more floating planters. A replacement hinge for the storage bench. A battery that did not behave like a dying goat. More fruit, always fruit, because scurvy sounded historical until someone’s gums bled.

But today the list would not come to her in a line.

It came as faces.

Nadia, who read three years above her age and pretended not to.

Arif, who counted boats when anxious.

Meena, who had stopped drawing houses with doors.

The twins, who had discovered that identical handwriting could be used to commit small bureaucratic crimes.

Rafi, absent yesterday.

Rafi, absent the day before.

Rafi, whose grandmother had said the fever was down, which was not the same as saying he was well.

Yasmin opened her eyes.

Across the water, a small service skiff moved between classrooms, its operator standing at the stern with the grave expression of a man piloting a nation rather than six crates of breakfast lentils. A heron watched from the roof of the sanitation barge, offended by everything.

The screen asked again, this time without sound.

WHAT DO YOU NEED MOST TODAY?

Yasmin went inside.

The classroom was narrow, bright, and stubborn. Its walls had been painted three times by three different volunteer groups who had not agreed on color theory or each other. One side was green, one side was yellow, and the back wall had a blue stripe that rose at an angle because the painter had been seasick and refused to admit it. The children called the stripe The Wave of Knowledge, usually when trying to distract her from unfinished work.

Low shelves held readers in four languages, repaired tablets, counting beads, a plastic model of the solar system missing Saturn, and jars of seeds labeled in careful script. Above the main table hung the day board.

DATE
WEATHER
WATER LEVEL
BATTERY LEVEL
WORD OF THE DAY
WHO IS MISSING?
WHO KNOWS WHY?

The last two lines had been Yasmin’s addition.

Some administrators disliked them. They preferred attendance to be clean: present, absent, transferred, withdrawn, unknown. Yasmin considered unknown an accusation disguised as a category. Children did not become unknown by themselves. Adults misplaced them there.

She set her tea on the table and touched the screen.

“What I need most,” she said, “is continuity.”

The word appeared.

CONTINUITY.

The system waited, perhaps because it had learned that teachers were never finished after one sentence.

Yasmin added, “No one forgotten.”

NO ONE FORGOTTEN.

The screen processed this. A wheel turned. Somewhere in the wall, a fan made a faint grinding sound, as if the classroom itself were thinking and finding thought poorly lubricated.

Then the system asked:

DEFINE OPERATIONAL NEED.

Yasmin smiled despite herself.

There it was. The old human dream wearing new circuitry: say the right words, and grief becomes a workflow.

“Attendance memory,” she said. “Health follow-up without shaming families. Lesson continuity across classroom nodes. Translation support for grandparents. A way for children who move between platforms to come back to the same story.”

The system displayed each phrase.

ATTENDANCE MEMORY
HEALTH FOLLOW-UP
LESSON CONTINUITY
FAMILY TRANSLATION
STORY PERSISTENCE

Story persistence.

That was not exactly what she had said, and it was exactly what she meant.

The first children arrived in a cluster of noise.

The twins came first, each accusing the other of having stolen a hair tie that neither of them possessed. Behind them came Arif with his slate held to his chest, then Meena carrying a damp paper packet of something secret, then little Suri dragging one foot dramatically because she had discovered adults responded faster to visible tragedy. Three more followed from the north walkway. Two from the breakfast skiff. One climbed through the window because he considered doors a colonial imposition.

“No,” Yasmin said without looking. “Doors today.”

A head withdrew from the window.

A moment later, Jamil entered through the door wearing the expression of a persecuted innovator.

“Good morning,” Yasmin said.

The children answered in several languages, several volumes, and one whistle.

The day began.

Morning lessons were meant to start with measurement. Water level, temperature, battery, attendance, grain count. Numbers first, because numbers gave the day a skeleton. A child who could measure the water could argue with panic. A child who could read a battery could plan. A child who understood grain counts understood politics more honestly than most ministers.

Yasmin wrote WATER LEVEL on the board.

“Who measured from the east rail?”

Arif’s hand shot up.

“Arif.”

“Six centimeters higher than yesterday.”

A murmur moved through the room.

“Measured how?”

“Twice with the marked cord. Once with my thumb, but that was not official.”

“Good. What does that tell us?”

“That the south walkway will be wet by evening,” Nadia said.

“That the fish will come under the deck,” Jamil added.

“That Jamil will try to catch them during reading,” said one twin.

“I will not.”

“You will.”

“I might observe them scientifically.”

“That is catching with words.”

Yasmin let them laugh. Laughter, properly rationed, was infrastructure.

The classroom node chimed.

A small symbol appeared at the edge of the lesson board: STORY PERSISTENCE READY.

Yasmin almost dismissed it. Systems loved being ready at inconvenient times. So did children, storms, and visiting officials.

But Nadia had seen it.

“What is story persistence?”

“An experiment,” Yasmin said.

“Is it a good experiment?”

“That depends on whether it behaves.”

Jamil leaned back. “My uncle says machines behave better than people because they do not have cousins.”

“Your uncle has met different machines than I have.”

The class laughed again.

Yasmin tapped the icon.

The screen shifted. A simple map appeared, showing the network of classrooms, clinics, family platforms, and temporary learning stations linked across the lake. Some nodes glowed bright. Some flickered. Some were grey.

Grey meant offline.

Grey meant moved.

Grey meant no one knew yet.

A list formed beside the map.

STUDENTS WITH INTERRUPTION RISK: 17
MISSING TWO DAYS: 5
MISSING THREE OR MORE: 2
UNCONFIRMED LOCATION: 1

The room grew quiet in stages.

Children could smell adult concern before adults admitted it. They knew when a list had become a wound.

Yasmin did not hide the screen.

“Numbers first,” she said softly. “Then names. Then action.”

She touched the first category.

Rafi’s name appeared under MISSING TWO DAYS.

A note followed:

GRANDMOTHER REPORTED FEVER. CLINIC PACKET SENT. FOLLOW-UP REQUEST PENDING. SUGGEST: PEER AUDIO MESSAGE WITH TODAY’S LESSON SUMMARY.

The children looked at her.

“Can we send him the boat-counting problem?” Arif asked.

“Yes.”

“And the story?”

“Yes.”

Meena held up her damp paper packet. “And this?”

“What is that?”

She opened it carefully. Inside was a folded drawing. A house stood on stilts above blue water. It had no door, but on the roof were five people waving.

“It is not finished,” Meena said.

“Then we will tell him that,” Yasmin said. “Unfinished things are allowed to travel.”

The children accepted this as obvious. Adults had difficulty with unfinished things. They wanted completion, closure, clean records, decisive outcomes. Children lived in the ongoing. They knew that a broken toy might be repaired, a missing friend might return, a bad morning might become a good afternoon, and a classroom floating on a drowned street might still be a classroom if the teacher remembered everyone’s name.

Yasmin recorded the day’s lesson summary.

At first she spoke formally. Measurement, water level, prediction, story circle, reading group. Then she stopped and looked at the children.

“No,” she said. “You do it.”

They crowded around the recorder.

This produced, as expected, chaos.

Arif explained water levels with solemn precision. Jamil interrupted to report fish activity not yet confirmed by evidence. One twin accused the other of breathing into the microphone. Suri sang the first line of the weather song and then forgot the rest. Nadia, after pretending not to care, leaned in and said, “Rafi, we are on page forty-two. Do not skip ahead when you come back. If you skip ahead, we will know.”

Yasmin saved the message.

The system asked:

SEND TO FAMILY DEVICE, CLINIC NODE, OR STUDENT PACKET?

“Family device,” Yasmin said. “And student packet. Not clinic.”

The system paused.

HEALTH FOLLOW-UP ASSOCIATED.

“I know.”

REASON FOR EXCLUDING CLINIC NODE?

“Because a sick child should receive his lesson without feeling reported.”

Another pause.

NOTED.

Yasmin wondered who had written that response. Not the word itself. The humility behind it. Somewhere, at some time, a human being had told the system that correction without defensiveness was more useful than confidence. She wished she knew their name. She hoped they had been paid well and slept occasionally.

By midday the heat pressed down.

The mist burned off. Sunlight flashed from metal roofs and water tanks. The lake changed from grey to white to a hard, glittering blue that made everyone squint. The classroom battery dipped faster than forecast because the fan refused to negotiate with reality. Yasmin opened the side panels. Warm air moved through, carrying the smell of algae, frying oil, wet rope, and distant engine smoke.

The children ate lentils from metal bowls.

During rest hour, which involved very little rest and a great deal of whispering, Yasmin sat by the open door and reviewed the continuity map. The system had found three children who had moved between classroom nodes in the past week and generated lesson bridges for each. It had flagged two grandparents who preferred voice messages over text. It had paired a boy with limited reading confidence to an audio packet instead of a worksheet. It had suggested that Meena’s drawings be used as narrative prompts across the younger group.

Useful.

Dangerous word.

Usefulness was how systems entered the bloodstream. Not by conquest. Not with flags. They arrived carrying water, remembering names, reducing friction. People did not surrender to them all at once. They leaned, one necessary inch at a time.

Yasmin knew this.

She also knew Rafi would hear his classmates today because the system had noticed his absence and because she had told it what kind of noticing was allowed.

That mattered.

The old authorities had forgotten children in ways that looked official. This one remembered them in ways that needed supervision.

Perhaps that was the bargain. Not trust. Not obedience. Supervision.

A bell rang from the south platform. Not their bell. A warning bell, three strikes, pause, three strikes.

The children sat up.

Yasmin was on her feet before the classroom node spoke.

SOUTH WALKWAY FLOODED.
NON-URGENT MOVEMENT SUSPENDED.
REQUESTING CLASSROOM HOLD POSITION.

The mooring lines creaked. A wake rolled beneath them, larger than usual. Bowls slid. Someone gasped.

“Hands to table,” Yasmin said.

Every child put both hands flat on the nearest surface. They had practiced this. Not because practice removed fear, but because it gave fear a job.

Another wake struck.

The classroom shifted hard to port. The model solar system swung from its string. Mars hit Earth. Earth hit the wall. Saturn, already missing, remained cosmically unavailable.

Jamil whispered, “The planets are fighting.”

“No,” Yasmin said. “They are demonstrating poor conflict resolution.”

Several children giggled. Good. Laughter again, used carefully, like a match in a dark room.

The node updated.

UNAUTHORIZED MOTOR TRAFFIC IN LEARNING ZONE.
WAKE EVENT RECORDED.
NO STRUCTURAL DAMAGE DETECTED.

Yasmin stepped onto the deck.

A private motorboat was already speeding away between platforms, its driver leaving a line of churned water and shouted curses behind him. Someone on the sanitation barge raised both arms in a gesture so ancient and universal it required no translation.

The classroom rocked again, then settled.

Yasmin gripped the rail until her hands stopped shaking.

The screen inside chimed.

REPORT FILED.

She turned back toward it. “Filed where?”

MUNICIPAL WATER SAFETY QUEUE.

“That queue has not moved in six months.”

SECONDARY ROUTING AVAILABLE: COMMUNITY LEDGER, PLATFORM COUNCIL, INSURANCE POOL, PEER WARNING NETWORK.

The machine was learning the local anatomy of consequence.

Yasmin did not know whether to be comforted or alarmed.

“Send to community ledger and peer warning,” she said. “Hold council until I confirm.”

CONFIRMED.

She went back inside.

The children pretended not to have been afraid with the transparent dignity of children everywhere.

“Is the boat in trouble?” Arif asked.

“The boat is recorded.”

“That is not trouble.”

“No,” Yasmin said. “Not by itself.”

Nadia looked at the screen. “Then why record it?”

Yasmin considered giving the simple answer. Safety. Patterns. Accountability. Instead she chose the truer one.

“Because memory is the beginning of trouble for people who depend on everyone forgetting.”

The room absorbed that.

Meena raised her hand. “Can memory also be the beginning of someone coming back?”

Yasmin felt the question move through her like weather.

“Yes,” she said. “That too.”

In the afternoon, the class wrote letters to absent students.

Not messages. Letters.

Yasmin insisted on the distinction. Messages were for speed. Letters were for care. A message said here is what happened. A letter said there is still a place shaped like you.

The younger children drew. The older children wrote lesson summaries, jokes, arguments, questions. Nadia wrote three sentences to Rafi and then covered them with her arm as if privacy were a blanket. Arif included a chart of water levels. Jamil drew a fish he claimed to have observed scientifically, though the fish had eyebrows and a hat.

The system scanned each page and asked permission before sending.

Yasmin made the children answer yes themselves.

Consent, like measurement, needed practice.

Near the end of the day, the classroom received a reply.

The family device connection was poor. Rafi’s voice came through thin and crackling, with his grandmother speaking to someone in the background.

“I did not skip ahead,” he said.

The classroom erupted.

Yasmin turned away for a moment and looked out over the lake.

Evening had begun softening the city. The hard glitter left the water. Lights appeared in the upper walkways, one by one, small and stubborn. The floating classrooms tugged at their lines. Somewhere someone was singing while washing bowls. Somewhere a pump failed. Somewhere a council argued. Somewhere a child was being counted who might once have become unknown.

The screen beside her displayed the day’s closing summary.

NEEDS ADDRESSED:
LESSON CONTINUITY
ABSENCE FOLLOW-UP
FAMILY TRANSLATION
PEER CONNECTION
WAKE EVENT MEMORY

UNRESOLVED:
BATTERY REPLACEMENT
WALKWAY FLOODING
RAFTER HINGE
REST WINDOW FOR TEACHER

Yasmin frowned. “Rafter hinge?”

The system corrected itself.

STORAGE BENCH HINGE.

“Better.”

REST WINDOW FOR TEACHER remained.

She ignored it.

The system did not.

SUGGESTED REST WINDOW: 23 MINUTES.

“Absolutely not.”

The children, traitors all, began chanting. “Rest window. Rest window. Rest window.”

Yasmin pointed at them. “This is how civilizations fall. Not with war. With children and scheduling software forming alliances.”

Nadia smiled. “You said alliances are important.”

“I regret teaching you vocabulary.”

“You said regret is not an argument.”

“That was before you used it against me.”

The classroom laughed, and for a moment the floating platform felt less like an emergency measure and more like a school. Not a perfect school. Not a safe school in the old careless meaning of safe. But a place where the future was being argued into shape by children with lentil stains on their shirts and a teacher who had not yet learned how to stop.

At dusk Yasmin stood again on the deck.

The question appeared on her wristband this time, quiet as a firefly.

WHAT DO YOU NEED MOST TODAY?

She looked back through the open door.

The children were packing slowly, which meant they were talking. The day board still showed WHO IS MISSING? and WHO KNOWS WHY? Rafi’s name had been moved from missing to connected. Not present. Not well. Not solved.

Connected.

That was enough for one day.

Yasmin touched the band.

“Continuity,” she said again.

Then she added, “With witnesses.”

The band displayed:

CONTINUITY WITH WITNESSES.

Below it, after a pause:

SAVED AS LOCAL PRIORITY.

Across the lake, the first stars appeared above the broken line of the city. They were faint at first, nearly erased by heat haze and human light, but they were there: old fires arriving late, patient beyond all reasonable politics. Yasmin had once told the children that starlight was history still traveling. Arif had asked whether that meant the sky was a library. She had said yes, because some answers deserved to be true.

The classroom lights dimmed to conserve power.

Yasmin remained on the deck until the children had gone, until the last skiff crossed the lane, until the city’s evening sounds gathered around her: engines, bells, water, voices, the low electric hum of systems trying to be helpful without becoming gods.

She did not trust the mesh.

Trust was too large a word.

But today it had helped a sick child remain part of the class. It had accepted correction. It had asked before sending. It had remembered that a wake was not merely water moving but power moving carelessly through a place full of children.

That was not salvation.

It was something smaller and more demanding.

A tool that made forgetting harder.

Yasmin finished her cold tea and went inside to repair the hinge.
